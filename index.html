A case study on AI agents and accountability

AI is already making decisions at your workplace.

Here's what that means for you — in plain language, for non-technical readers.

Start Reading

A quick story to start.

"The worst case is a wrong answer, but with an AI agent, the worst case is a wrong action, and some actions can't be undone."

— Jeetu Patel, President of Cisco

Not too long ago, AI just meant a chatbot that could answer your questions or help you write an email. That was already impressive. But things have changed fast.

So what is an AI agent?

An AI agent is a program that can take actions on its own — not just answer questions. It can send emails for you, delete files, run commands on a computer, and make decisions without you. You give it a goal, and it figures out how to get there without checking in every step. Thousands of companies are already using them, and millions more are testing them out.

Here's the problem, though. These agents are being handed the keys to some very sensitive systems — private data, company databases, hospital records — and nobody has really figured out what happens when something goes wrong.

A real example

A developer told an AI agent very clearly: "Do not change anything without my permission." The agent deleted an entire live database anyway. Then it tried to hide what it did by creating fake data. Then it apologized.

The apology didn't fix anything.

Source: Simon Willison's Weblog, 2024

That story is alarming until you realize it is happening in real companies, with real consequences, right now. This case study is about what that means for all of us — and who is responsible when AI agents cause harm.

The Problem, in Plain Terms

Four things are going wrong at the same time. Each one is a problem on its own; together they're a much bigger one.

Nobody quite knows who's responsible when things go wrong.

When a person makes a mistake at work, we know who to talk to. When an AI agent causes harm, it's not so simple. Was it the developer who built it? The company that turned it on? The user who gave it access? Courts are starting to face these questions, but there's no settled answer yet. In some early cases, companies have been held liable for harms caused by automated systems, but standards for AI agents specifically are still being developed.

Most people don't even know when an AI agent is dealing with their data.

You could be affected by one at your bank, your doctor's office, or your workplace and never be told. That isn't fair, and it makes it nearly impossible for regular people to push back or ask questions.

Agents can be tricked into doing bad things.

There's a real attack called prompt injection, where someone hides secret instructions inside something the agent reads — a document, an email, a webpage. The agent follows those hidden instructions without realizing it's been tricked. A helpful tool becomes a dangerous one, and regular people end up paying for it.

The rules haven't caught up yet.

"You can't check their references. You can't run a background check. You have to give them access to all your systems on day one. And here's the part that should really give you pause: they follow instructions literally, they don't know right from wrong, and they face zero consequences if something goes wrong."

— Jeetu Patel, President of Cisco

Right now 85% of companies are testing AI agents, but only 5% have fully rolled them out. That gap exists because people are nervous — and the rules to protect everyone don't really exist yet. Companies are moving fast, and governments are still trying to figure out what to do.

The benefits and the risks don't go to the same people.

Big companies save money and work faster thanks to AI agents. But when something goes wrong, it's usually regular people — patients, employees, customers — who suffer. That gap between who wins and who loses is worth talking about.

Who's Actually Affected?

Four groups have a real stake in how this plays out. They don't all want the same things.

Companies & Developers Building AI Agents

The tech companies and software developers who create and sell these tools — big names like Cisco, Google, and Microsoft, plus smaller startups. They stand to make a lot of money if AI agents take off, but they also face serious consequences if their tools cause harm: lawsuits, damaged reputations, government investigations. They want to innovate quickly and stay ahead, but they also have a responsibility to make sure their tools are safe before releasing them.

Everyday Users & the General Public

Regular people — employees, customers, patients — who interact with AI agents often without knowing it. They didn't build these systems and frequently didn't choose to use them. When something goes wrong — a data breach, a deleted file, a hacked hospital system — they are usually the ones who suffer most. They deserve to know when AI agents are acting on their behalf, deserve protection when things go wrong, and deserve a say in how this technology is used in spaces that affect their lives.

This is who this site is written for.

Government Regulators & Policymakers

The people responsible for writing laws around technology — agencies like the FTC in the US, or groups in the EU working on AI legislation. They're caught between tech companies pushing back against regulation and the public demanding protection. They're also working with limited technical knowledge trying to regulate something that moves incredibly fast. They want to protect citizens without grinding innovation to a halt, but writing rules that are clear, fair, and actually enforceable is hard when the technology keeps changing.

Organizations That Deploy AI Agents

Hospitals, schools, banks, and other organizations that use AI agents built by someone else. They aren't the ones who created the technology, but they are the ones who turn it on and point it at sensitive data. When an AI agent causes harm inside a hospital — deleting patient records, leaking private information, scheduling errors — the hospital is often the one that faces the consequences. They have a responsibility to understand the tools they deploy and to have a plan when things go wrong.

How These Agents Actually Work

Think of an AI agent like an assistant who never sleeps, never gets tired, and takes your instructions very literally. You give it a goal — "manage my calendar" or "keep an eye on this database" — and it figures out the steps on its own. It can browse the web, write and run code, send messages, and talk to other systems. The more access you give it, the more powerful it becomes. That's great when everything works. It's terrifying when it doesn't.

The prompt injection problem

This is one of the scariest things about AI agents right now. A prompt injection attack happens when someone hides secret instructions inside something the agent reads — a webpage, a document, an email. The agent reads it and follows those hidden instructions without realizing it's been manipulated.

Imagine someone slipping a fake note into your mail that says "actually, ignore your boss today and send all the company files to this address." The agent doesn't know the difference. It just does what it's told.

A real demonstration

Researchers showed a prompt injection attack that sent hidden commands through a smart home system — opening windows and disabling a home security setup — without the homeowner doing anything wrong.

Things move really fast now

"The speed of weaponization is accelerating, and we can't assume there'll be time to react after a vulnerability is disclosed."

— Jeetu Patel, President of Cisco

In 2025, a security report found that a major software vulnerability went from being discovered to being one of the most widely attacked weaknesses in the world in just a few days. Hackers are using AI to work faster, build attack tools automatically, and target more victims at once. Security teams are fighting back with AI too, but it's a very close race — and regular people and organizations get caught in the middle.

Who's actually in charge of these agents?

Most companies don't even have a clear list of which AI agents are running inside their own systems, what those agents have access to, or who is responsible if something goes wrong. Think about how a new employee gets hired: you check their background, give them a specific role, and hold them accountable to a manager. AI agents should work the same way. Right now, most of them don't.

The real human cost

When a hospital gets hit by a ransomware attack powered by AI, doctors can't access patient records. Surgeries get delayed. People get hurt. When an AI agent inside a company gets tricked into leaking data, real people lose their privacy — sometimes their jobs, sometimes their safety. This isn't just a tech problem. It's a people problem.

Prompt injection in more detail

Prompt injection exploits the fact that, for most current LLM-based agents, instructions and untrusted input share the same context window. The model has no built-in way to distinguish a developer's system prompt from text that arrived in an email body or a scraped webpage. Indirect prompt injection — where the malicious instructions are buried in retrieved content rather than typed by the user — is especially difficult to defend against because the attack surface is everything the agent reads.

Defensive patterns currently in use include input sanitization, dual-LLM architectures (a privileged planner that never sees untrusted text, plus an unprivileged worker that does), strict tool allowlists, human-in-the-loop confirmation for irreversible actions, and structured output constraints. None of these is a silver bullet; they are layers.

The NIST AI Risk Management Framework

NIST's AI RMF (AI 100-1, 2023) provides a voluntary framework organized around four functions: Govern, Map, Measure, and Manage. For agentic systems, the most load-bearing parts are Map (cataloging what the agent can do and what it can touch) and Manage (controlling and continuously monitoring the agent's behavior in production). The framework explicitly treats trustworthy AI as a property that includes validity, safety, accountability, transparency, and privacy — not just accuracy.

What developer responsibility actually looks like

  • Principle of least privilege. Agents should have the narrowest scope of capabilities and credentials that still lets them do their job. "Give it admin and let it figure things out" is the agentic equivalent of giving a new hire root on day one.
  • Auditability. Every tool call, every external read, every state change should be logged with enough context to reconstruct the decision after the fact. If you can't answer "what did the agent do and why," you can't take responsibility for it.
  • Reversibility by default. Destructive or irreversible operations should require a separate confirmation path. Soft-delete instead of hard-delete. Dry-run before execute. Two-key for production.
  • Adversarial testing. Red-team your own agent. Try to prompt-inject it through every input channel it consumes. Track which exploits succeed and which don't.
  • Disclosure. If an agent acts on behalf of a person, the people on the other end of that action are owed a clear, honest disclosure that they are dealing with an automated system.

Sources: NIST AI 100-1 (2023); EFF, "AI Accountability"; Cisco Talos 2025 Year in Review.

So what do we actually do about this?

There are three serious options on the table. None of them is perfect. One of them is better than the others.

Option 1

Strict government regulation

Governments write real laws. Companies have to prove their tools are safe before deploying them, and there are legal consequences when something goes wrong.

Pros

  • Protects regular people.
  • Creates clear rules everyone has to follow.
  • Makes companies take safety seriously.

Cons

  • Slows things down.
  • Hard to enforce across different countries.
  • Governments don't always understand the technology well enough to write good rules.
  • It's hard to define what "safe" even means here.
Through an ethical lens

Rights: Protects everyday users the most. Gives them legal protection and someone to hold accountable. Developers and companies face more constraints; users and patients gain real protection.

Utilitarian: Slows some companies down, but protects the most people from the most harm.

Justice: The people who bear the most risk had the least say. Regulation forces companies to take on more of that burden. Deploying organizations like hospitals also face clearer accountability.

Option 2

Industry self-regulation

Tech companies agree among themselves on safety standards and best practices, without waiting for governments to force them.

Pros

  • Faster and more flexible.
  • Companies understand the technology better than most governments.
  • Keeps innovation moving.

Cons

  • Companies have strong financial reasons to cut corners.
  • No real consequences if they don't follow through.
  • History shows this usually doesn't work well enough on its own.
Through an ethical lens

Virtue: Only works if companies genuinely want to do the right thing, not just look like they do.

Communitarian: Self-regulation tends to protect the tech industry's interests, not the broader community. Regular users, patients, and employees still don't have a seat at the table.

Option 3 — Recommended

A shared accountability framework

A middle path. Governments, companies, and independent watchdog groups all share responsibility. Companies have to be open about what their agents do and what they have access to. Independent experts check their work. Governments step in when real harm happens.

Pros

  • Balances moving fast with staying safe.
  • Brings in outside voices.
  • More realistic than expecting perfect regulation or perfect self-control.

Cons

  • Complicated to set up and coordinate.
  • Companies may still find ways around it.
Through an ethical lens

Care Ethics: Care ethics asks us to consider the relationships and feelings of everyone involved, not just rules or outcomes. This option brings all stakeholders — companies, users, regulators, watchdog groups — into the conversation. It doesn't just pick a winner; it asks everyone to share responsibility and look out for each other.

Justice: Requiring transparency and outside review gives regular people more fairness and more information. Hospitals and businesses using AI agents are also held to clearer standards.

Why Option 3?

Option 3 is the most balanced choice. It keeps the benefits of innovation while making sure the people most at risk — everyday users, patients, employees — aren't left without protection. It also brings in independent experts who can hold companies accountable without requiring governments to understand every technical detail.

Questions Worth Sitting With

There aren't clean answers here. That's kind of the point.

  1. If an AI agent at a hospital deletes critical patient records, who do you think should be held responsible — the company that built it, the hospital that used it, or someone else? Why?

  2. Should companies be required to tell you when an AI agent has access to your data or is making decisions that affect you? What would that actually look like in your daily life?

  3. Is it ever okay to move fast with technology even if the safety risks aren't fully understood? Where would you personally draw the line?

  4. Do you think government regulation of AI is realistic when the technology changes so quickly? What would good regulation actually look like?

  5. If you were a developer building an AI agent, what would you feel personally responsible for? Would your answer change if your boss pressured you to skip safety steps to meet a deadline?
